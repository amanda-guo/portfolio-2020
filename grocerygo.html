GroceryGo

Android Grocery Shopping Application

Tools

Role

UI/UX Designer & Front-End Android Developer

Project Overview

GroceryGo is an Android application that recommends the cheapest location for users to buy their groceries. We start by allowing users to create a custom grocery list and then our algorithm generates a recommendation in their local area. This project was completed in a team of 4 students.

Problem

Students are always running out of time. We noticed this with ourselves, but also with our friends who are usually following strict schedules to get everything done before a deadline. What was odd about this was how first year university students seem to feel busier than upper year university students even though their course load is easier. We decided to dig into this issue.

Upon further investigation, we realized that many first year students are not overwhelmed with the school work they needed to complete, but are struggling more with tasks that they never had to do when they lived with their parents. This included chores, laundry and buying food for themselves. For many first year undergraduates, this is the first time they have lived on their own and independently. Figuring out how to balance weekly tasks and school work is an area that takes time to get accustomed to, especially when they are living in a new area. This is where we were able to create a solution.

The issue with the transition from high school to university was that students had to get accustomed to living on their own. This meant they needed efficient ways to complete weekly tasks, such as laundry and grocery shopping.

Initial Brainstorming and Sketches

We realized that grocery shopping is a task that is quite cumbersome for these first year students. Not only do they need to figure out a weekly budget for how much they want to spend on groceries, but they also need to figure out where they can buy their groceries without going over their budget. This is where we put our heads together to brainstorm the solution.

We focused on targeting the planning stage of a weekly grocery shopping experience. By creating an application that allows a user to create a custom grocery list, we used those items to generate the cheapest location in the area for the user to buy their groceries.

Before any development, we started by discussing the screen flow for the application. We decided to focus on a select few screens that gave users a simple and quick way to key in their information, create their shopping list and let our system tell them the best location to visit. The focus was creating a quick user journey (after all, we wanted our application to save them time). This was a key component for me to consider when creating the low fidelity wireframes later on.

Low Fidelity Wireframes and User Testing

Next, I always create low fidelity wireframes to get the placement of each component on the UI, and really focus on the flow from one screen to another. I keep in mind the user's perspective during this stage, as my goal would be to create a cohesive and consistent experience from start to finish for the user. For example, each task the user has to complete is modularized into its own separate screen to keep it simple for each person. This shows a straightforward CTA for each page of the application.

For user testing, I asked various people to try out the prototype of the application, and asked them what they thought about the user experience. I got some feedback on the placement of the buttons, as well as including some valuable information like the total estimated price at the end with the recommendation. I iterated on the initial design and produced the following wireframes.

High Fidelity Design and Development Integrations

For the high fidelity design, I did some online research to look for best practices on how to place elements on each screen. I took inspiration from general application login pages and list views. I tested many different placements of the elements on the screen before coming up with this final design. For example, I used background images to visually show the user going through the shopping experience. The first image displays the user's goal of getting their groceries. Then, the next screen has a picture of the groceries in a store to show the browsing phase as the user types in their information. While they select their items, there is a picture of an empty shopping cart in the background to illustrate that action. Finally, when they get their recommendation, the screen looks like an e-receipt so it shows a final conclusion (and goal) of their shopping trip and experience on our application.

I also took over the UI development of this prototype on Android Studio. Along with the front-end development on Android Studio, this was integrated with our Firebase database to query stored grocery prices. We used UiPath to map to web pages that display the prices for groceries at individual stores and then dynamically refreshed the data for real time updates.

Reflection

The biggest challenge was to understand the problem, figure out a solution and develop a product in only 36 hours. We had to move quickly, make final decisions and iterate on our designs with as much information as we had at the moment in order to keep the project moving forward. Also, at the beginning of the project, we were strangers to each other as we had only just met. In the end, we worked hard together and created an application we were very proud of.

One valuable lesson I learned was why so much discussion happens between the designers and the developers. It’s because designers consider the ideal and developers consider the practical - we had to find a middle ground between the two so both ends could be satisfied. Given more time, or a second time, I would like to go through more iterations with each screen. A big part of the design process is the multiple iterations and comparing designs side-by-side to decide which one is ultimately preferred. I would have liked to do this more (and that’s where I would seek more feedback from as well).

Also, one feature that would have enhanced the UX of the application would be to allow the user to search for items to add to their grocery list. Given the time constraint, we used a check-list to develop an MVP, but the search functionality would enhance the user journey.
